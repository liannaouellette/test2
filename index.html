Seasonal College Meal Plans







What is a meal plan?


A meal plan is a seven-day program
in which you put together a list of
foods suiting your pallet with the
idea of having your best nutritional
needs in mind. A meal plan may be
followed by certain people with a
specific objective in mind, such as
losing weight, lowering cholesterol
improvements, achieving nutritional
targets, saving time, etc. There are
also others such as athletes and
such who create a meal plan
to ensure they get enough nutrients
to perform. There are different types
of meal plans, some make their decisions
based on a specific health condition
they have, such as diabetes and so forth.
Meal plans are created generally by
dietitians or health care workers, but
lately creating meal plans at home is on
a rise. As time progresses, people are
starting to become more and more aware
of their health and meal planning is a
way to ensure that they stay on track.



Some meal plan considerations:

When making a meal plan, there are things you should consider before taking your first step towards choosing your meals For Example repetition is key in the creation of a meal plan. That being said, you can mix it up occasionally. You don't need to eat the same meals every day. I’d recommend choosing 2-3 different breakfast, lunch and dinner meals to eat throughout the week while mixing in some healthy and satisfying snacks. Switching up your source of protein can allow for some variety in your meal plan. Switching it up between chicken, eggs, beans, meats, protein powder etc, can allow your meal plans to be versatile while still getting that satisfaction you’ve been looking for. Cooking in bulk is another thing you should consider before meal planning. If you're all set for the week and are planning on having oatmeal with fruits 3 mornings throughout the week, instead of making it every morning, spend a couple extra minutes to prepare your meals in advance and store it in your fridge until you are ready to eat it. One last thing to keep in mind is that it's okay to have a cheat meal once a week. It's okay to order out and eat a burger, but you need to keep that in mind while making your meal plan because the last thing you want is to be throwing out food because you made too much.



Benefits

While for some, meal planning may
seem useless and a waste of time,
there are actually tons of benefits
that come with it. Everyone has their
own reasons for meal planning, no
matter their goals in which they are
striving to achieve, everyone can
find meal plans beneficial. Having
your meals for the week planned and
ready to prepare can spare countless
thoughts about what to eat, and the
stress and loss of time cooking and
planning meals come with. This could
especially be beneficial for people
who work long hours, manage health
conditions such as diabetes, or with
family to feed. Furthermore, meal
plans can help you have a healthier
food selection and fix your eating
habits. It is scientifically proven
that when we are hungry and our
blood sugar levels drop we tend to
impulsively buy food that we enjoy
as pizza or hot dogs out of convenience.
With meal plans and a proper grocery list,
you will be sticking to the healthier
alternatives such as vegetables, whole
grains and beans in comparison to the
healthier alternatives such as chicken,
rice and beans. There’s no doubt that eating
healthier is beneficial for everyone,
but it is especially beneficial for
those with type 2 diabetes or heart
problems. Having a meal plan to stick
to can lessen the stress put upon
consumers and make eating healthy an
experience far more enjoyable and less
overwhelming.



Shopping Tips

  • Keep a grocery list handy in the kitchen and jot down what you need as soon as you run out. Before taking a trip to the store, take note of what you already have and only buy what you need.

  • Avoid shopping when you are hungry to prevent any impulse purchases and overbuying.

  • Most grocery stores keep the most expensive (and sometimes least healthy) options on the shelves at eye level. Explore the entire shelf before deciding on what to buy. Often, a generic brand of any product can be found nearby and is less expensive.

  • When running low on fresh ingredients, try experimenting with different combinations of shelf-stable foods. For example, adding canned tuna and frozen broccoli to boxed macaroni and cheese is an easy balanced meal! Turn a can of beans into a burrito bowl by combining it with rice, salsa, cheese, and lettuce or sautéed peppers.
  • Keep an eye out for when pantry items are on sale and stock up!

  • Maintain an organized pantry by storing grains and dried legumes in clear jars. Try keeping similar items, such as baking ingredients, in a basket or separate drawer.

  • Where you shop matters. Stores such as maxi and IGA are great for having better prices and deals, as well as fresh produce. Try to mix in a monthly trip to Costco to buy your bulk items such as olive oil, oats, and flour can save some extra money while you're at it.
